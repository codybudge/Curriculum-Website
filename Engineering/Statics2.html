Statics 2 - Strong Structures

Step 1

FORCES

A Falling Rock


To make the strongest structures, you need to know about forces. So what is a force? Well, Gravity is one good example. Gravity acts on everything in the world. If you dropped a rock, gravity would pull it down until it fell all the way to the ground. If we make a diagram of a falling rock, it would look like this.

BUT THEN WHAT HAPPENS? Once the rock stops, does gravity stop working? What does the diagram of a rock resting on the ground look like?

A Resting Rock

So even when the rock is still, there are forces pushing and pulling it. The force from the ground is called a “reaction” force, because it reacts to the force of gravity. If the ground couldn’t resist as much as gravity was pulling, the rock would keep moving down.

Let’s replace the ground with a structure.

Build the Post and Lintel Bridge

1. Build the A-Frame Posts

Attach two Angle Girder 120's to an Angle Girder 7.5, and place Angle Block 7.5's on the bottom. Repeat this step four times (You should end up with 4 A-Frames total).

2. Finish the Posts

Cross X-Strut 84.8's beneath the I-Strut 60's. Attach I Strut 30's to the sides, and are parallel to the ground.

3. Complete the Bridge

Assemble the lintel with 3 Angle Girder 120's on each side. Use a Spring Cam to attach the ends without a Pin.

Results

What happened? Does the bridge change in any way? Since it supports the rock, the diagram would look similar to the diagram earlier.

The bridge is strong enough to provide a sufficient reaction force for the weight of the rock.

What about the bridge?

The force of the rock on the lintel pushes down on the posts.

The reaction of the ground is split between both posts, but the total reaction is equal to the force of the rock.

The force that the posts experience is called "Compression", like when a spring is compressed.

Force that would pull a spring open is called "Tension"

A Closer Look

When the rock is resting on the bridge, it causes the bridge to sag. When objects are loaded with a heavy force, this can happen. Too much of this will cause a catastrophic failure! If we take a look inside the Lintel, we can see what kinds of forces are reacting ON A MOLECULAR LEVEL!

The bend caused by the opposing forces causes a different reaction to the top and the bottom of the lintel.

The Yellow line represents the maximum compressive force.


The Purple line represents the maximum tension force.

As long as the tension force on the bottom of the lintel isn't enough to break it, the lintel will hold and the bridge will support the force of the rock.

Analyzing the Break

In this test, the force of the rock was much greater than the reaction force the bridge could provide.


But what happened that caused it to break?

A Closer Look

The force of the rock was enough to cause the lintel to fail. When lintel fails, it is normally due to a break in the bottom of the lintel material.


Failure in a structure like this is called "Buckling".

Strengthening the Bridge

It's possible to make this bridge support the large rock. If the lintel is strong enough to resist the tension in the bottom, it will hold. There are multiple ways of doing this.

The thicker a lintel is, the more resistant it is to buckling, but adding more weight to the bridge can cause it to fail, too.

(This is not the way.)

Trusses

To make the lintel thicker AND reduce the weight as much as possible, we'll make a truss. A truss has an upper and lower frame that is secured with internal supports. This reduces weight AND increases strength.
