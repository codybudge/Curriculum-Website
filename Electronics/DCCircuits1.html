ATOMS AND EVERYTHING

Background on Atoms

Everything in the unverse is made up of atoms. Combinations of atoms are what make up materials. For example, steel is made up of Iron and Carbon atoms. Water is made up of Oxygen and Hydrogen atoms.

Parts of an Atom

Atoms have three parts: Protons, Neutrons, and Electrons. Each of these does a different job.

Neutron

Neutrons have mass, and increase the weight of the atom. They are in the center (or the Nucleus) of the atom.

Proton

Protons are like Neutrons. They are in the nucleus and have the same mass as neutrons. Protons also have a positive electric charge.

Electron

Electrons are the only part not in the nucleus, and they donâ€™t give the atom extra mass. They do have a negative charge, however.

Protons cannot move from atom to atom, which is why different elements are classified by the number of protons they have.

Moving Electrons

Electrons and Protons have opposite charges. Just like with magnets, opposite electrical charges attract and pull together, while the same charges repel and push themselves apart. ELECTRICITY

Shocking Information

Electricity is the energy of charged particles. This energy is released when electrons move from one atom to another. Electricity can be a steady stream from a supply of electrons, or a single release of built-up electrons.

STEP 4

HANDS-ON ELECTRICITY

Grounded in Reality

All charge tries to "even out" by reconnecting with the other charge. So any electrons that you have stored up will take the fastest route back to being neutralized. If there isn't another opposing charge this will mean that it has to go somewhere big enough to handle it, and there's nothing in the world bigger than... THE EARTH. Charge will try to work its way back to the "Ground".
Lightning rods are a device that is meant to help protect homes from lightning strikes using this principle. A metal rod reaches above the house, and a wire runs into the ground, causing the lightning to bypass the house.

STEP 5

OHM'S LAW

Voltage

Aside from Static Discharge, electricity can move through a circuit. A Circuit is a closed loop where electrons can move from a negative terminal to a positive terminal, like the (+) and (-) sides of a battery! In fact, batteries are measured for this reason using something called Voltage.

Voltage is a measurement of the difference in electric potential between the positive and negative ends. Voltage is labelled with a 'V', and measured in units called 'Volts'

Increasing Voltage will cause more electrons to flow through a circuit every second.

Current

The amount of electrons flowing through a circuit will determine how much charge is flowing through per second. This measurement is called the Current. The more Current there is, the more charge is flowing through.
Current is, for some reason, labelled with the letter 'I'. So when you see 'I' in a diagram, know that it means current.

Resistance

Some materials are better at allowing electricity to flow through them. These materials are called Conductors. Materials that are not so good are called Insulators. When you stood used the box to stay off the ground, you were 'insulated' from the ground.
Resistance is the measurement of how difficult it is for electricity to flow through something. Insulators are very resistant, while conductors are not very resistant.

Circuits

To be a complete circuit, there must be Voltage (V), Current (I), and Resistance (R). If one of these things changes, it will effect the others. The way these affect each other is called "Ohm's law". Check out how it works with the simulation below.
